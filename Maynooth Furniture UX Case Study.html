Prologue

User Profile and Brief

Before I go into this UX case study, I want to be clear that this was a project that I carried out in my spare time through a course on the website Udemy. The specific course is: User Experience Design Essentials - Adobe XD UI UX Design. The course covers the basics of the User Experience process. It also teaches you how to use Adobe XD as a UI design and prototyping tool. There are two different projects involved in the course that the students partake in. This project is self-guided, but the course as well as the materials provided gave assistance when needed throughout the project’s timeline. Those materials are a project brief and a persona brief.

The project’s brief and requirements are as followed:

  • The project’s client is Maynooth Furniture. A furniture seller who is looking to have an e-commerce site built for them.
  • The brief includes a feature list of required pages and content within them. It also includes a list of two competitors to Maynooth furniture, habitat.co.uk and made.com.
The project’s targeted user/persona is detailed here:

  • Katherine is an executive at the local offices of an international medical device company. She earns a salary of $110,000 per year. She has children at University. She is both style & price conscious. Katherine is updating the furniture at the 4-bedroom family home. The update in furniture is following the slow remodeling of the house now that her kids have left for university. She loves to browse style guides online. She uses Pinterest to gather her design ideas.
  • So quick review: Our targeted user is an affluent, successful woman whose children are now adults. She is looking to re-furnish her house since her kids are now away at college. She is price and style conscious. She likes to create Pinterest style boards that help her with inspiration for shopping ideas.
Since this project was a requirement for an online course, I attempted to follow the process of creating a unique experience for the targeted persona. But it came with its’ own unique challenges.

Discover

Identify Needs

Even though I had been given the needs of the project right off the bat in the brief, I still needed to teach myself how to use the Adobe XD. The project took me through a basic understanding of how to use it. Since I have used the Adobe Suite products (specifically Illustrator and Photoshop) it came to me a bit faster than normal. I also had taken courses on front end development languages which also helped me understand how to lay out my pages. This led me to my research on this project.

Research

Compare Competitors, Identify Faults, and Find a Real-Life User Profile

Now the course had led me down a path of creating mid-fi wireframes, prototyping, picking fonts, picking colors, optionally customizing the given logo for Maynooth, creating a mood board, and creating a full color desktop mock-up. I was feeling confident in my ability to use Adobe XD and I was interested to see what someone would think of the mockups on the message board sections of the course. I posted my links and waited. Other than the occasional “Great!” from someone, I received little to no constructive criticism. I decided to not stop there. I currently work for a webhosting company, so I decided to reach out to our UX/UI Design department. I spoke to two separate designers. They were both helpful in the sense of answering my questions about the field. But one was willing to meet with me via Zoom to answer some questions I had and give me some advice on my designs. Shako met with me two times after our original meeting and stressed to me that UX Design is very research based. The problem he saw with my designs was that I was not thinking the way that my persona would. He suggested I that investigate psychography and its place in UX Design. He also suggested that I make at least three versions of the mid-fi wireframe navigation bar. I did my research on psychography and understood that to improve qualitative data in my design I would need to find someone who fit the bill for the persona.
I found that my Mom fit the bill quite well. Not perfectly, but she was a great source of information to refer to when I began my second and third drafts for the desktop mockups. I also sat down with her for some A/B testing too.
For the initial mid-fi wireframes, I was primarily researching the listed competitors in the brief. But after I had spoken to Shako, he stressed performing more research on other websites that targeted the persona’s demographic. I then made a list of websites that I could use as a reference for some design inspiration. I have them listed here:

  • Westelm.com
  • Wayfair.com
  • Nordstrom.com
  • AnnTaylor.com
  • Anthropologie.com
Nordstrom’s navigation bar was primarily inspiring for my mid-fi wireframe drafts. I felt that the least cluttered and straightforward navigation bar would be an improvement over the cluttered nav bars of the competition in the brief. After I had created multiple drafts of the nav bar, I felt it would make more sense to move on to the color drafts and customize the layouts. I created multiple drafts of the layouts of the home and product listing pages.

Test

A/B Test with Real Life User Profiles

After I had created at least three drafts of the required pages, I decided to A/B test with my Mom. I let her click through the prototype. I wanted to see what parts of it jumped out to her. She complimented the navbar at first. She liked that it was not cramped with information. She did not have a lot to say about the homepage, the contact page, or the product detail page. But she did have a lot of good feedback on the product listing page. While she could understand the information being presented, she preferred the titles be at the top of each product. The first piece of information she wanted to see was what the product was called as opposed to the product itself. She explained that this would help her remember it in case she forgot to favorite it. It also helped her differentiate between the products when the title is above the picture. She then commented on the filtering options. She preferred the sidebar as opposed to the buttons with dropdown options at the top, surprisingly. The reasoning was “that it looks better”. My assumption was because it was left aligned and due to its size, it was not very distracting, but it is big enough to draw attention. She preferred the text in the product listing to be center aligned along with the color options. This was most of the information I gathered in my notes for that A/B test.
While I felt somewhat fulfilled, I thought it would be a good idea to test with at least two more people. The problem was that I did not know a lot of people that would fit the user profile perfectly. I instead thought it would be a good idea to test with some female friends of mine to see what they thought to get a slightly different perspective on the mockups. Now my friends did not fit the age or income bracket of user profile, but I felt that they could understand what I was presenting to them and give me some good feedback. I invited them over to my apartment and sat them down to look at my prototypes. They were not too focused in on the home page, but they were having trouble understanding the buttons on it. This I chalked up to creating some improvements on the hero sliders on the top and bottom of the page. I showed them the three different search filter options on the product detail page and asked which they would prefer. One preferred the sidebar option (the same option my mom preferred) and the other preferred the button style fixed sub navbar option. She liked that option because she was used to the filters being at the top of the page when searching for items on an e-commerce site. This gave me a good solution to the filtering option on the page since the side bar now outweighed the top filters two to one.

Design

Creating the Mid-Fi Wireframes and Full Color Mockups

When I first started the course there was no emphasis on lo-fi, sketched wireframes of the pages that were being asked to be designed. Instead, I jumped right into mid-fi wireframing in Adobe XD. This got my feet wet using the software, but I would normally start out with some sketches first and then move on to a design software. After I had shown Shako my mid-fi wireframes, he suggested I go back to the drawing board and make at least three different nave bars based on my research. I made three drafts of the nav bars based on the listed websites that I thought had the least cramped and was the most functional. I wanted to create one that was clear and easy to understand as opposed to the competition. Once I had created the three drafts, I decided to stick with one to continue with the full color mockups. I created three drafts for the home page and the product listing page of the full color mockups too.

Navigation Bar

The main goal I had for this was to keep it from being too cluttered. I found two recurring designs while I was performing research on other e-commerce sites. First, they seemed to be overly complicated and second the search option was often centered. My solution was to have a right centered search bar that is hover activated. To clear some of the common clutter, I made the account link a dropdown with the idea that encouraged creating an account with the option of a favorites list still available to guest users, but that information is cleared if an account is not created. Finally, I included a cart item and a sub-nav with all the item categories of the website.

Footer

I left the footer to be very plain and nondescriptive. It does not deviate far from the norm because I received little to no feedback on it.

Home Page

The home page consists of what is asked in the brief with a couple more sections. Starting with the hero slider I thought it would make the most sense to begin with a straight to the point call to action to bring the user into a section of the website. Within the slider is a further explanation of the living room essentials: sofas and armchairs with a button to lead to the product listing page. Immediately after the slider is the clearance deals section starting with the sofas and armchairs deal again. The other sections of the site follow with the other clearance deals to the right of it with higher percentages. I tried to follow the Gutenberg principle after the user scans from the hero slider down to the clearance deals. I repeated this idea with the simple graphic in the middle of the page and followed it up with a new in store section. Finally, I included a social media section at the bottom of the page with a Pinterest logo due to our persona’s preferences.

Product Listing Page

I found that the feedback I received after A/B testing was primarily focused on this page. When I first made the initial drafts, I provided multiple filtering options. I also had a couple different alignments for the text and the product information. The most important pieces of information I received after testing was the preferred filtering option and product information layout.

Product Listing Cards

The user was focused first on the pictures, but then they wanted to know the product titles second not the price. They also had trouble differentiating the products from each other since there were no boundaries between them in the drafts. I fixed this by adding a subtle box shadow around each product card and presenting the title of the products directly above the pictures. After the product picture, I have the color options before the price based on the user’s focus being the product themselves. Finally, I have a link out to the product details page’s review section at the bottom of each card in the star ratings. Following through with the user’s affection for creating Pinterest boards, I have a favorites option in each card as well.

Filtering Options

The favorite filtering option was the sidebar. The reasoning behind this was originally “just because I like it”, but I suspect that the Gutenberg principle can be applied here as well since the sidebar was left aligned. I gave it a fixed position for a better view when scrolling through the products and I left the product listing at three per row to give a better view of the products too.

Favorites Page

I added a favorites option since most e-commerce websites have a wish list. I wanted to have something closer to the website Asos technically, but not aesthetically. I liked their simplistic system and I thought it work for this project. You click the heart icon, it fills, and the item is added to your wish list. It is basically the exact same page as the product details page except some different filtering options. An idea that I thought would add to the user’s preferences when it came to Pinterest boards was an alternate view for the favorites list. This view would use the alternate pictures available from the product details page and list them in a tile style.

Product Details Page

The first section has alternate images of the product in a hero slider and the main product information to the right of it. The product title and price are the largest pieces of that column. They are followed by an anchor link to reviews section below. I found after testing and research that if the user is going to this page to begin with, they are going to want to find out as much information on the item as possible. So, I thought that an anchor to the reviews section would bring them to the bottom of the page and force them to scroll up past the product details section with collapsible portions. This would encourage the user to collect information before they add the item to their cart. I also thought I would add some comical reviews to it too. After the reviews anchor, I have a short blurb in lorem ipsum beneath it, the color options, and then the add to cart button.

Contact Page

I wanted to go with a separate contact page as opposed to the other options of a contact form such as a modal that comes into the foreground while the home page is in the background because after testing, I thought it would be best. While my user profile brief might not have a preference, I understood the psychography of who I tested with and knew that a separate page was the best option. I understand that it might be quicker to have the modal option, but with the separate page it takes out some possibility of the user being overwhelmed with what is happening on the page and provides familiarity with the header and footer being able to be clearly seen. It is also slightly easier to input information. I also created the cat graphic to add a little style to the page and eliminate white space.

Conclusion

Final Thoughts

In summary, I am confident I met what was asked in relation to the project of the course. I provided each page with every requirement. I even went the extra mile and added a few more pages to add more depth in the end.

Positives

I think some of the positive aspects of this project were that it helped me understand the UX process better. It helped me understand how to use Adobe XD and create UI design mockups that I could use in my portfolio. One of the most important parts of this project was that it spurred my interest in learning more about this field. I wanted to research and learn from my mistakes, so I could get better. And this project provided me a no risk scenario to test out what I could do in using these tools.

Growth Points

Some areas in this specific project that I felt I could have improved upon were prototyping and testing. Testing being the more important and uncontrollable. There were many parts of the UX process that I missed in this project, but I still understand their importance. Such as working one on one with a user and a developer. I never underestimated the importance of a report with those contributors to the process and I understood that working on a course like this would not hit every part of it completely. I only tested with three people because I could not find many willing to contribute their feedback in the middle of a global pandemic (I started this project in 2020). When I spoke to Shako and researched testing online, the minimum number of users tested is normally five. I did not quite hit that mark. But if this were a project that was fully fleshed out, I would have made a point to do that. Prototyping was not the greatest since I was not too focused on the moving parts as opposed to the designs themselves. I felt that there some parts of the designs that would have looked a bit better with some animations such as the filter sidebar on the Product Listing Page. I thought that making it collapsible would have been interesting. Overall, I am somewhat happy with the design, but I would like to work on more boundary pushing designs or projects with younger user profiles. There is always room to improve and make every design better. But I am confident that I delivered a product that met the user’s needs for an e-commerce website.
